## Thesis Risk Assessments

- The strongest risk is overgeneralization. The collapse papers robustly support the claim that naive or indiscriminate recursive training on synthetic data destroys tails and diversity. However, multiple counterexamples show that this is not equivalent to 'synthetic data cannot substitute for human data.' Curation, correction functions, negative-guidance use, and modified optimization rules all create real exceptions or at least plausible escape hatches. At the same time, the medical contamination result shows those escape hatches may not transfer to safety-critical, rare-event domains. A careful thesis should therefore say: curated or verified synthetic data can sometimes substitute for fresh human data under strong selection/correction assumptions and task objectives, but fresh human data remains hard to replace when preserving rare tails, clinical variability, or open-ended distributional support is essential. Verified large-scale real-data filtering is also a serious competing alternative mechanism.
- Overall risk is moderate. The literature does support measurable temporal change and some concrete contamination signals, but the strongest evidence is either domain-specific (privacy policies, Wikipedia) or pipeline-specific (C4). High-performing filtered-web corpora such as RefinedWeb and FineWeb are the main counterweight: they show that even if the web is drifting, strong filtering and selection can preserve or recover model-relevant quality. So Beat 2 is safest if stated as 'the live web shows measurable change and emerging degradation pressures' rather than 'the web is broadly and already degrading in a way that directly reduces pretraining usefulness.'
- The main risk is not a clean refutation that human data is useless; it is that several strong papers show scalable substitutes and alternative mechanisms. RLAIF and AlpacaFarm are serious counterevidence that commodity human preference labeling can often be replaced by AI feedback. DPO and RRHF show that optimization design, not just data provenance, can explain alignment gains. The safest thesis is therefore narrower: human data remains most valuable where the target itself must be grounded in authentic, diverse, truth-sensitive, or demographically representative judgments, and where AI feedback is only
- Moderate. The strongest synthetic-data and AI-feedback papers support a substantial reduction in reliance on human-authored supervision for instruction following, chat quality on standard benchmarks, and some preference-learning workflows. However, several papers impose real scope limits: human post-editing still improves perceived quality, human-labeled data remains important for safety-specific alignment, demographic representativeness is not guaranteed by AI judges, and model scale plus small curated human datasets provide an alternative explanation for some gains. A thesis that claims human supervision can be largely reduced is defensible; a thesis that implies it can be broadly eliminated is at significant risk.
- The strongest evidence in this set does not support a universal contamination-to-quality-decline claim. RefinedWeb, FineWeb, and Yi are substantial scope limiters: they show that aggressively filtered, deduplicated, and scaled web corpora can outperform curated alternatives. The opposing papers do identify real problems, but they are more conditional and mechanism-specific: C4 documentation shows hidden contamination and biased filtering, Wikipedia shows polluted subdomains, Retrieval Collapse shows exposure amplification in live retrieval systems, and Consent in Crisis shows authenticity/consent failures that may not appear as immediate benchmark decline. Overall risk to a simple thesis is high: it should be rewritten from 'web contamination straightforwardly causes quality decline' to 'web contamination creates serious but pipeline-dependent risks, and filtered/documented corpora can partially offset performance decline while leaving authenticity, fairness, and consent issues unresolved.'
- moderate_to_high. The J papers give credible evidence that post-training data composition strongly affects general alignment and chat quality, but the strongest I papers show that these gains do not automatically transfer to social reasoning or Theory-of-Mind benchmarks. Later social-specific work further limits the thesis by showing that some fine-tuning can worsen False Belief behavior and that latent-capability/steering explanations remain live alternatives. The safest claim is not that 'data composition broadly solves social reasoning,' but that post-training composition is one plausible lever whose effects on socially grounded reasoning remain under-validated and may depend on task-specific, human-grounded data rather than generic alignment corpora.
- Moderate-to-high. Multiple papers offer genuine alternative mechanisms to training-data-quality explanations: DEL-ToM shows inference-time scaling can improve ToM without retraining, and CoSToM argues ToM knowledge may already be latent and merely need better externalization. However, these alternatives are often benchmark-specific and do not yet clearly solve richer pragmatic, cross-cultural, or bias-sensitive social reasoning. The safest thesis posture is not that authenticity is the only driver, but that it remains an important and possibly complementary driver once strong inference-time baselines are controlled for.
