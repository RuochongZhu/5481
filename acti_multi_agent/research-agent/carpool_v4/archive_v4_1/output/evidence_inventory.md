## Beat 1: Small-Town Campus Transportation & Coordination Gaps

A narrower rural and small-town transport coordination problem is documented in the A-category mobility literature, while peer-platform trust friction is documented in the B-category trust literature. Together this motivates a small-town campus transport coordination question and points to a plausible reason students might turn to peer-organized rides. The corpus does not directly establish that small-town university students are underserved by commercial ridesharing, so Beat 1 should stay at the level of contextual motivation.

- **Use to say the mobility coordination problem is documented in rural integration research.**
  Role: background motivator
  Finding: Shows that rural mobility access often depends on coordination across multiple actors and modes; it supports a coordination-heavy setting rather than a campus-specific rideshare sh
- **Supports the narrower claim that small-town and rural access is often organizationally complex.**
  Role: background motivator
  Finding: Compares institutional designs for rural public transport access and indicates that service sufficiency is shaped by governance and integration choices.
- **Useful for operational fragility, not for student-specific unmet demand.**
  Role: background motivator
  Finding: Indicates that sparse-demand shared mobility in rural settings faces bundling and demand-management constraints.
- **Supports campus transport salience, but not peer-rideshare scarcity.**
  Role: campus-adjacent contextualizer
  Finding: Documents that campus-bound riders care about specific public transport attributes and that campus access quality can vary.
- **Use to explain why peer coordination can face trust frictions.**
  Role: trust contextualizer
  Finding: Shows that profile photos act as trust signals in stranger exchange settings.
- **Supports the claim that participation frictions are partly trust-mediated.**
  Role: trust contextualizer
  Finding: Finds that reviews, identity verification, and social-media-linked cues shape perceived trust in sharing platforms.

Remaining gaps:
  ⚠ Direct campus-specific study of rideshare scarcity in rural or small-town universities is missing.
  ⚠ No paper directly shows that campus populations compensate through peer ride coordination.
  ⚠ Campus evidence is transit-quality oriented, not peer-rideshare-gap oriented.
  ⚠ Motivation remains contextual rather than direct evidence for Beats 4-5.
  ⚠ The corpus can motivate a narrower claim that rural/small-town mobility systems are coordination-heavy and often imperfect, with general peer-platform trust frictions layered on top. It does not directly establish that small-town university students are underserved by commercial ridesharing or that campus populations specifically compensate through peer coordination.

Be explicit that the institutional-identity strand is the weakest in the provided set: the best available evidence is adjacent identity/trust work, not direct .edu or campus-scope studies. ## Beat 7: Adversarial Scoping

Keep the tone explicitly concessive: use formulations such as 'may reproduce,' 'we acknowledge,' and 'scope-limited.' Do not convert any paragraph into a defense or mitigation claim. Paragraph 2 should directly name the sample skew (82% Mandarin-native; English-native students N=15) and admit that the cited evidence is indirect: it shows demographic heterogeneity in platform outcomes, not language-specific campus carpool behavior. Paragraph 3 must state that no real-world deployment compared the four design decisions, so no claims should be made about improved uptake, safety, fairness, or retention. Paragraph 4 should separate identity verification from competence, conduct, and governance. Paragraph 5 should say that points may invite gaming or fabricated activity; the support is adjacent platform evidence, which should be acknowledged as analogous rather than definitive.
